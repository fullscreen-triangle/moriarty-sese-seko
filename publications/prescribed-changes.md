Paper 1: The Purpose of a Character
Fix 1: Floor theorem proof — one sentence addition
In the proof of Theorem 3.1, replace the current justification with: a zero-thickness boundary is not a boundary — it fails to separate — so every boundary that actually separates has positive thickness, hence β > 0. This removes the dependence on the infimum argument entirely and grounds the floor in the definition of distinction itself.
Fix 2: Non-locality of identity needs a theorem, not just a witness
Currently Theorem 4.1(iii) is established by a single constructed example (two triangles). The claim needs either: a theorem showing non-locality holds generically under stated conditions, or an honest demotion to: "identity is non-local whenever the minimising partition is not a singleton split, which occurs whenever the graph has a cheaper multi-block cut than any singleton cut — as in the following construction." The two-triangle witness then demonstrates the condition exists, but the claim is no longer that non-locality is a necessary consequence of the axioms alone.
Fix 3: Identity and character need to be two distinct objects
As established in our discussion of the boundary/interior distinction: identity (Γ(A)) is the minimising partition — where the cut lives, defined from inside. Character (χ(A)) is the boundary structure at that partition — the cut as seen from outside. These are two orientations of the same object. The paper currently uses χ as both. Add a definition separating them and a theorem showing they are two faces of the same cut. This resolves the structural problem that character is what other agents find invariant, while identity is what generates it — they are the same cut seen from opposite sides of the boundary.
Fix 4: The drive field definition does not actually select
Definition 5.3 defines a drive field as a probability-like weighting. Theorem 5.4 then defines a selecting drive as the special case giving positive weight to exactly one edge. The theorem proves selection requires this special case, but the proof is circular — it defines selection as the thing that selects. Replace with: show why the gating field is the only structure that could resolve underdetermination, by arguing that any rule using data beyond (V,E,w) is encodeable as a gating field, and any rule not using such data fails by Lemma 5.1. This makes the necessity argument non-tautological.
Fix 5: Attractor theorem scope mismatch
Theorem 6.3 proves exponential convergence for the continuous gradient flow. The corollary about robust character applies this to the discrete gated walk. Add an explicit discretisation argument: show that the gated walk is a discretisation of the gradient flow with step size bounded by the act floor β_a, and that the convergence rate λ applies to the walk within a constant factor determined by the step size. Without this, the rate λ cited in the corollary belongs to the flow, not the walk.
Fix 6: Society-level purpose needs a drive potential
The society graph Σ satisfies the three axioms by Lemma 7.1. But the society-level purpose is defined as a region P* in outcome space with tolerance τ — a target, not an attractor. The agent-level purpose is an attractor derived from a strongly convex drive. For self-similarity to be genuine propagation of the invariant rather than formal coincidence, add: a society drive potential Φ_Σ on a society disposition space B_Σ, strongly convex, whose unique minimiser maps to P* under the collective outcome map. Then prove this minimiser is an attractor by the same argument as Lemma 4.2. The water-filling law at society level is then the expression of this attractor under finite collective budget, and self-similarity is the statement that the attractor structure survives the quotient.

Paper 2: The Physiology of Response
Fix 1: Same floor proof as Paper 1
Axiom 3 (response floor) currently just posits β > 0. Add the one-sentence grounding: a zero-cost response deposits nothing and changes nothing, so it is not a response — it is the absence of response. Therefore every genuine response costs at least β > 0. This makes the floor constitutive of what a response is, not an independent axiom.
Fix 2: The consistency graph H is ungrounded
Definition 3.3 introduces a consistency graph H with vertices as disposition components and edges carrying transports θ_ij. No axiom or argument establishes that the agent's internal structure is a consistency graph of this form. H appears fully formed alongside the disposition space B, and the relation between them is never stated. Add either: an axiom stating that the agent's disposition components carry a transport structure, with the consistency graph as its formalisation; or a construction deriving H from the self-graph of Paper 1, showing that the holonomy structure arises from the same internal boundary costs. Without this, Theorem 4.4 (coherence testable in advance) and Corollary 4.5 (agent can refuse destructive response) both float.
Fix 3: Threshold policy proof covers the wrong problem
Theorem 3.2 proves the threshold policy is optimal for the fractional knapsack — responses divisible into floor-sized units. But responses are drawn from a finite repertoire (Definition 2.4) — they are discrete, not divisible. The integer knapsack is NP-hard in general. Add either: a proof that the response repertoire has sufficient structure (e.g. responses are additive in cost and gain) to make the fractional solution integral; or an honest statement that the threshold policy is optimal for the continuous relaxation and approximates the true optimum up to one boundary item. The corollary about token responses (Corollary 3.1) inherits this gap and needs the same qualification.
Fix 4: Phase exclusion axiom is unmotivated
Axiom 4 (construction and commitment draw on the same instantaneous capacity) introduces "instantaneous capacity" — a different object from the attention budget α, which is defined over a unit interval. Instantaneous capacity is never defined. Add either: a definition of instantaneous capacity as the limit of the attention budget as the interval shrinks to a point, with an argument that this limit forces exclusion; or ground phase exclusion in the self-graph structure of Paper 1 — forming a new distinction alters (V,E,w), while committing a response applies a map on B, and these cannot both occur at the same instant because altering the graph changes the domain of the response map while it is being applied.
Fix 5: Irreversibility conflates two claims
Theorem 5.1 proves two things: committed count is strictly increasing, and responses deposit cost ≥ β in expended attention. These track different quantities. The count is a timestamp. The expended attention is a running total. Neither is a change in disposition x. The claim "a revisited disposition is never a revisited state" is true only because state includes the count — it is definitional, not because responding genuinely changes the agent in a non-trivial sense. Either: add a theorem showing that responses also change disposition in a non-trivial way (x shifts by at least some function of β), making the irreversibility genuine rather than merely counted; or explicitly acknowledge that non-return is a consequence of the committed count being part of the state, and that two agents at the same disposition but different counts are distinguished by timestamp alone.

Structural Problems
Fix 1: The floor proof has the same infimum gap
Theorem 2.1 sets β := inf_v σ(v) and asserts β > 0 because each σ(v) > 0. Same error as every other paper in the series. The fix: the world-graph is finite (|V| < ∞), so the infimum is over a finite set of positive reals, hence a minimum, hence attained and strictly positive. Add this sentence to the proof. Without it, the floor — which the entire paper's argument turns on — is not properly established.
Fix 2: Axiom 1 (distinction floor) is stated as independent but Theorem 2.1 derives it
Axiom 1 posits β > 0 directly. Theorem 2.1 then proves β > 0 follows from non-completability. These are in tension — you can't both posit something as an axiom and then derive it as a theorem in the same paper. Resolve this one of two ways: either remove Axiom 1 and let Theorem 2.1 do the work (the non-completability axiom alone is enough), or keep Axiom 1 and demote Theorem 2.1 to a remark explaining that the axiom is not independent — it follows from the more primitive non-completability condition. The current structure gives the floor twice with different epistemic status, which is confusing.
Fix 3: The recognition-search identity (T1) is circular in exactly the same way as Paper 5
Axiom 2 (purpose-directed search) defines the agent to access its world only by search — fetch-access is excluded by definition. Theorem 3.1 then proves recalling and searching are one operation. But this follows immediately from the axiom. The theorem is a tautology. The interesting argument — which needs to be added — is why fetch-access would break the agent's character. The argument: a fetch returns an answer as it was stored, not as the agent's current graph and committed history determine it. But the world-graph changes with every committed act (the monotone record grows). So a fetch from an earlier state returns an answer appropriate to a prior agent — one the current agent cannot re-occupy by Theorem 4.1 (monotone non-return). Fetch-access is therefore architecturally incoherent for an agent with a monotone history, not merely excluded by definition. This argument should replace or supplement the current proof.
Fix 4: Path opacity (T2) proves less than it claims
Theorem 4.1 shows same-seed, same-report searches have differing interiors, and the report doesn't determine the interior. The proof is correct. But the corollary (agent cannot certify its own account) draws a stronger conclusion: the agent holds the report but not the search that produced it, and cannot recover the search. This requires an additional premise that the agent stores only the terminal report, not the full walk history. That premise is never stated. Add it explicitly: the agent's state is the pair (current disposition, committed count) — it carries the terminus and the count, not the interior of the walk that produced the terminus. Without this premise, a sufficiently sophisticated agent could in principle store its walk history and recover its route.
Fix 5: Receiver-relative report (T3) conflates two distinct claims
Theorem 4.2 proves the same event registers different resting cuts in agents with different world-graphs, and each is correct in its own graph. Clean. But the corollary says divergence is not error in either agent — and then Remark 4.3 says a true account and a false account are indistinguishable in the producing agent's state. These are different claims. The first (divergence is not error) follows from T3. The second (true and false accounts are indistinguishable) requires the additional argument from T4 (falsity is undefined inside the agent). The corollary is running ahead of the proof. Either move the indistinguishability claim to after T4, where it's earned, or add a forward reference making clear it depends on T4.
Fix 6: The falsity theorem (T4) has a standard that appears from nowhere
Definition 5.1 introduces a "standard" account a* against which falsity is defined. The standard is described as "another agent's account, a world-state read-out, a consensus." But world-state read-out assumes there is a privileged view of the world — a view not subject to the same floor and receiver-relativity that applies to every other agent. This is inconsistent with the framework. If every agent individuates the world against its own medium (T3), then a "world-state read-out" is itself a receiver-relative registration, not a privileged standard. Either remove "world-state read-out" from the options, or add an argument that the standard is always another agent's account with no claim to privilege — which is what Theorem 5.2 (falsity is a relation on a pair) establishes anyway. The inconsistency is minor but it undermines the clean conclusion that falsity is never absolute.
Fix 7: The drift theorem (T5) assumes independent reshapings without justification
Theorem 6.1 proves the surviving fraction along a relay chain is the product G_n = ∏ g_i, citing "independence of the reshapings." But why are the reshapings independent? Each relay runs its own search on its own world-graph. If relay i's world-graph overlaps substantially with relay i+1's world-graph — which is likely in a social network where agents share much of the same world — then the reshapings are not independent: relay i+1's search is constrained by the same world-structure that constrained relay i. The product formula requires genuine independence. Add either: a condition under which independence holds (e.g. the agents' world-graphs are disjoint, or the account passes through sufficiently different graphs that correlations are negligible), or an honest statement that the product formula is an approximation whose accuracy depends on the degree of world-graph overlap between relays.
Fix 8: The forced inquiry theorem (T6) conflates forcing and selection
Theorem 7.1 proves a further act is forced by the positive residual gap D > 0. Theorem 7.2 proves the content of the forced act is selected by the gap. These are presented as separate theorems but the proofs are not clearly separated. Specifically, Theorem 7.2 says the next act is "the search that most reduces D" — but this is Axiom 2 (purpose-directed search) applied directly. The theorem is just restating the axiom. What's missing is an argument that the gap-selecting act is specifically what an observer would classify as a question rather than as any other kind of act. The distinction between a question and a report is never formally established — both are D-reducing search termini by Theorem 3.1. Add a definition distinguishing question-acts from report-acts by their direction: a report-act responds to an incoming account (reduces D created by what was received), while a question-act initiates a new gap (creates D in the interlocutor). Without this distinction, the claim that questions are "forced by the gap" applies equally to all responses, making the specific result about inquiry vacuous.

Additions Needed
Addition 1: The agent is a proper part of an infinite whole
The paper never states what the world-graph is or where it comes from. Based on our conversation, the world-graph is not constructed or given as input — the agent is a finite subgraph of the infinite whole, and the world-graph is the visible portion of that infinite from the agent's position. Add to Section 2 a brief statement: the agent's world-graph is the finite subgraph of an infinite whole that the agent has individuated so far. The medium vertex m is not a separate object — it is the rest of the infinite whole, always already present, against which every item is told apart. Non-completability (Axiom 4) then follows from the infinite whole being genuinely infinite, not from any assumption about the world growing. This grounds the framework in Paper 8's foundational account and removes the appearance that the world-graph is an arbitrary given.
Addition 2: The vocabulary theorem needs a definition of testimony
Theorem 8.1 says testimony names an account — a search terminus — and is the one vocabulary item that attaches to an agent-output. But the paper never defines testimony formally. It's used in the abstract and introduction as an informal term and then appears in Theorem 8.1 as if defined. Add a definition: testimony is the act of emitting a report — a search terminus — in response to a query. It is the only named act because it is the only thing the agent does that is observable from outside (the search interior being path-opaque by T2). This definition makes Theorem 8.1(i) non-trivial: testimony attaches to the output but not to any intent, because the output is all that's observable and the interior (including any intent-like structure) is opaque.
Addition 3: Connect to the NPC architecture explicitly
The paper's results — emergence of testimony, lying, rumour, and questioning without representation — are the social-epistemological consequences of the NPC architecture from Papers 1, 2, and 5. Add a paragraph to the discussion connecting them: an NPC implemented under the four invariants of Paper 5 (conserved identity, monotone count, search-not-fetch, phase exclusion) automatically exhibits the behaviours this paper characterises. Lying without a liar, rumour without an author, questions without inquiry — these are not bugs or design choices. They are what the implementation invariants look like from outside when multiple such agents interact. The observer's vocabulary (lie, rumour, question) is the social-level description of what the individual-level architecture produces. This connection makes the paper not just a standalone result about embedded agents but the social completion of the NPC series.
Addition 4: The β > 0 grounding from boundary thickness
As established in our conversation: a zero-thickness boundary is not a boundary — it fails to separate. Therefore every boundary that actually separates has positive thickness, so β > 0. This should appear as a one-sentence justification in the proof of Theorem 2.1, replacing or supplementing the infimum argument. It makes the floor constitutive of what distinction means rather than a derived consequence of non-completability. Non-completability then does different work: it explains why the world-graph is always expandable (there is always more to individuate), not why each individuation costs something.
Addition 5: The locus theorem should include the agent's own acts
Theorem 8.1 locates testimony, lying, rumour, and questioning in the observer's vocabulary. But it doesn't address what vocabulary the agent itself uses — or more precisely, that the agent uses none of these terms for its own acts. Add a corollary: the agent classifies none of its own acts as testimony, lie, rumour, or question, because these classifications require holding a standard (for lying), tracking a chain of relays (for rumour), or representing an intent to inquire (for questions) — none of which are in the agent's state by T4, T5, and T6 respectively. The agent's self-description, if it has one, is exhausted by its current disposition and committed count. Everything else is the observer's addition.
